## Post 1: 9 advices in 17 years in sales 

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/galaga_ive-been-in-sales-for-17-years-and-managed-activity-7468291513035264000-AgKU/

I’ve been in sales for 17 years and managed 100s of AEs. This profession is so full of bad advice and it took me ages to find my mentors. Here are 9 things I wish someone had told me years ago…

1. Buyers Close Deals, Not You

It’s easy to obsess over sales meetings. But the real magic happens in buyers' internal meetings—those you’re not invited to. Align your actions with the critical steps THEY need to build consensus. Don't sell. Enable them to buy.

2. Your Sales Process is Meant to Be Broken

On that note, your role is not just to execute your process but to offer ‘Project Management Services’ to your buyers’ process. Your process is there to offer a good foundation to build on, but it only covers 5-17% of your buyers’ process.

3. Calendar Overload = Illusion of Control

Weekly champion syncs feel great, but the truth is—you’re probably not moving the needle much. If you fail to equip buyers between calls with the right tools or content, you’ll discover your “champion” isn’t championing. Help them build momentum internally—24/7.

4. Budget is Almost Never the Deal-Breaker

Stop asking about budget on the first call. It is rarely a reason to qualify out. No buyer only spends on pre-allocated line items. “No budget” simply means “No Justification” to create one for the value you’ve been able to convey.

5. Accessing Stakeholders is NOT Enough

Multithreading is not about having many people on your calls/emails. That’s a vanity metric. It’s about building separate relationships to support their specific needs/requirements/concerns. That’s what truly moves the needle.

6. Executives Hate Deep Discovery on Calls

Structured deep discovery doesn’t work well in executive calls. You must lead with stories, teach them something new, and let a conversation develop. They have zero patience. If you try to have long conversations first, they’ll tune off.

7. You’ll Fail 99% of Outbound Intro Calls if You Treat Them Like Inbound

You lead with discovery on Inbound but lead with insights on Outbound—It’s OK to use slides if they serve the discovery. It’s even OK to demo before you have all the pain points figured out. As long as it all serves the discovery.

8. Drop the ‘Perfect Discovery’ Fantasy

A perfect disco only works in a role-play. Try it in real life and you'll end up with no next steps. Worry less about your framework, and more about having a meaningful conversation around ‘why do anything’, ‘why now’, and ‘why you’. The rest only helps you and can come later.

9. Speed is Your Biggest Easy ‘Hack’ to Drive Urgency

Nothing cuts down deal cycles like dictating a fast rhythm of communication. Received an email? Answer from your phone right away. Talking next steps? Offer a call tomorrow. Discussed timeline/MAP? Recap that on every follow-up.

——

Don’t make it harder than it has to be.

Make mistakes. Learn from them.

But always learn from other people’s mistakes first.

## Post 2: AI sales tech reality

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/galaga_i-run-an-ai-sales-tech-company-and-i-can-activity-7465740904985780225-T43e/

I run an AI sales tech company and I can tell you AI agents are not replacing AEs in mid-market or enterprise sales. Not in 2028. Not in 2030. Maybe not ever.

Last week, a VP Sales asked me, straight up: would his AEs still have jobs in 3 years? He'd been ordered to build an 'agentic sales org' by EOY. And he was genuinely worried. My answer: AEs aren’t going anywhere.

Here's what most people keep missing about AI and sales:

1. Sales AI is 10x more complex than coding AI

Coding happens in a closed system. AI tests pass/fail in seconds. AI auto-corrects. Sales has none of that. You're not influencing a system - you're influencing 11 humans with conflicting agendas, politics, fears, while a competitor's AE plays the same game against you. 100s of touchpoints across email, calls, demos, async. Coding AI lets you iterate to perfection. Sales AI has to be right almost every time - or the deal dies.

2. The shift is not only technological, but sociological

Even if #1 was easy to solve, it’d still require deep behavioral change. Human buyers would have to accept the AI worker as a trustworthy alternative in this high-risk transaction, and dealing with tech in a situation where their careers are on the line. This is not a self-checkout at a supermarket…

3. Complex deals get decided in private channels that AI is locked out of

Even when that behavioral shift happens, real complex deal magic happens in places AI can't operate - a WhatsApp thread with a champion, a dinner with a CFO, a late-night text between execs. These aren't data gaps. They are real human moments that win deals today. No model upgrade fixes this. AI doesn't have a body.

4. Human buyers will make AI’s job harder over time

The moment AI got good at SDR emails, buyers learned to delete them. The moment AI gets good at negotiation, buyers will demand humans. The system gets harder as AI improves. Coding doesn't have this.

5. The economic model is brutal; AI has to be better than your $200K OTE reps

Autonomous BDR? You risk a lead list. Painful but recoverable. Autonomous AE? Now you're risking lost pipeline, lost deals, brand reputation, missed revenue. If I tell you I’ll get you human AEs at 10% the salary you’re paying, and 20% the results, will you hire them? Of course not. Which means AI needs to become as good as your B players for this to work.

——

But the part most sales leaders are sleeping on?

Agents should improve the QUALITY of parts of the job:

Research, account planning, stakeholder mapping, follow-ups, content, emails.

Or the parts humans hate: chasing stakeholders, key decisions, answering urgent buyer Qs at 11pm, surfacing risk, keeping momentum alive between calls.

That’s what we’re betting the future of Aligned on.

AI should be an ‘Iron Man suit’ for sellers.

Make buying easier. And selling smarter.

Not automation, or cost-saving “wins”.

Transactional sales will (and should) run autonomously on AI.

Everything else? 

Sellers stay. The tools change.

## Post 3: Top SMB AE performance

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/galaga_our-top-smb-ae-hit-176-in-q1-and-broke-all-activity-7462487314477408256-fxTK/

Our top SMB AE hit 176% in Q1 and broke all records. Even in Mid Market. Our VP Sales listened to EVERY single call to spot the pattern. All I could see was one thing:

He's not running a sales process.

He's running their buying process.

And while the below might sound "basic,"

That is exactly why it works.

I bet 95% of folks reading this don't do these 👇🏽

1. He's the slowest demo on the team

Most reps demo within 5-10min. He asks 8-12 questions first. Role. Priorities. Pain. Deal complexity. What success actually looks like. He won't touch the screen until the “oh, I know how to run this” lightbulb turns on. By the time he demos, the buyer is already half-sold from just the questions.

2. He never asks “so, tell me about your company.”

Most reps open with discovery questions the buyer has already answered 4 times to 4 different vendors. He walks in already knowing the org. The priorities. What’s happened in the deal. If new folks join, he brings a "what we heard" recap. The buyer never starts from zero. They feel respected, not interrogated.

3. He runs calls like a project manager, not a salesperson

Most reps wing it thinking "natural convos" wins. It doesn't. It just makes you feel good. The call’s going nowhere. He sends agendas. Defines outcomes per attendee. Every call ends with the buyer walking out feeling “this was time well spent.”

4. He never asks "any questions?"

That question gets you silence and dead air. He asks "what's landing?" or "what wheels are turning?" Open-ended. Forces a thought, not a yes/no. And gives him exactly what he needs to adjust the rest of the call in real time.

5. No call ends with "I'll follow up."

That phrase leaves the buyer hanging. Every call ends with a next step that facilitates buying. The deal doesn't lose energy between calls. Because there is no "between."

6. He says "no, we don't do that."

Most reps spin. "It's on the roadmap." "We’ll build it." Buyers feel ‘sold to’. He just says it as it is. Then either takes it to the team or refocuses on what we actually solve today. Trust goes up. The deal moves faster.

——

Look at the 7 again.

Not one of them is about persuasion, closing tactics, or charisma.

Every single one is about removing a reason for the buyer to drift or doubt.

That's the pattern.

The average rep still thinks the job is to convince.

The top rep makes convincing unnecessary.
